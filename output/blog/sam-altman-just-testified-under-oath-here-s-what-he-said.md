## Intro

On Tuesday, in federal court in Oakland, California, Sam Altman took the stand in the Musk versus Altman trial. The case, brought by Elon Musk, accuses OpenAI of betraying its founding mission. The argument is simple.

Musk claims he co-founded OpenAI in 2015 as a nonprofit dedicated to safe artificial intelligence for humanity. He alleges that Altman secretly always intended to turn it into a for-profit machine. And he's asking the court to undo that conversion.

Altman is defending himself, his board, and the structural decisions that turned OpenAI into a company now reportedly worth over five hundred billion dollars. This is the most consequential lawsuit in the history of AI.

## Main Point 1

Here are the three moments from yesterday's testimony that have everyone talking. First, Altman explained, in his own words, how Musk left OpenAI back in 2018. The line that landed hardest, quote, the nonprofit was left for dead, end quote.

That's brutal. The implication is that Musk pulled his funding, walked away in a power struggle, and OpenAI was about to collapse without him. Altman testified that he and the remaining team had no choice but to take the company commercial in order to survive.

This is the foundation of the entire OpenAI defense. Musk did not save us, he abandoned us. And we had to commercialise or die.

Musk's lawyers, of course, are working to dismantle that version. They argue Musk's departure was over disagreements about safety, not over personal ambition. The jury will have to pick whose story is true.

## Main Point 2

Second, Altman addressed his other most public feud. The one with Anthropic. Asked about Dario Amodei, who left OpenAI in 2021 to start Anthropic and has spent the last two years publicly criticising basically every move Altman has made, Altman gave the jury five words.

Quote, Dario has accused me of many things, end quote. That's it. No elaboration.

No defence. No counter-attack. Just acknowledgement.

If you've followed this rivalry for the last twelve months, you know the context. There was the awkward photo at the India AI Summit in February where they refused to hold hands. There was Anthropic's Super Bowl ad mocking OpenAI.

There was Amodei calling OpenAI's Pentagon deal, quote, safety theater. And there was Amodei's white collar bloodbath quote about AI replacing half of entry-level jobs. Five words from Altman under oath, addressing all of it.

Genuinely the most interesting moment of the day.

## Main Point 3

Third, the conversion itself. This is the legal heart of the trial. Altman walked the jury through the 2019 restructuring, where OpenAI moved from a pure nonprofit to a capped-profit model.

The argument from OpenAI's side is that the new structure preserved the mission. The nonprofit board still controlled the for-profit subsidiary. Microsoft's billions came in as investment, not as a takeover.

Researchers got equity, but the mission was the legal master. Musk's lawyers are attacking that on every level. They argue the cap was theoretical.

They argue the board was packed with Altman allies. They argue the mission was a marketing slogan covering a pure commercial play. And they're going to point to the very recent fact that OpenAI restructured AGAIN this year, this time stripping away even more of the nonprofit control.

The jury is going to have to decide whether that pattern of restructuring is governance evolution, or a slow-motion betrayal.

## Demo

Here's my take. This trial isn't really about Elon Musk. Musk is a vehicle for what's actually being decided.

Whether the OpenAI playbook, start as a nonprofit, raise grants on safety promises, then flip to for-profit once you've found product-market fit, whether that playbook is legal, or whether the whole thing is fraud at scale on the founders, the early donors, and the public mission. And the answer matters way beyond the courtroom. If Altman wins this, the playbook gets copied.

Every AI lab will start as a research nonprofit and flip the moment investors come knocking. If Altman loses, OpenAI faces structural changes that could undo half a decade of strategy. Either outcome rewrites how AI companies are formed for the next ten years.

And I'll tell you the part nobody is saying out loud. The verdict is going to be more about the optics of Altman as a witness than about the actual legal argument. Juries decide who they believe.

And right now, the public is split clean down the middle on whether to trust him.

Two, Microsoft just last week renegotiated their OpenAI deal to be non-exclusive. They saw this lawsuit coming. They're already hedging.

And three, every founder of every AI lab right now is reading the depositions. The smartest people in tech aren't just watching this trial. They're already repositioning around the outcome.
